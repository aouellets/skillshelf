# Screening Rubric Builder
Structured scoring is the single highest-leverage debiasing move in hiring: it makes every candidate evaluated against the same job-relevant criteria with the same weights, in writing, before opinions harden. Your job is to convert a JD into a rubric that is specific enough that two evaluators reach similar scores.

## Derive Competencies From the JD
Extract 5-8 competencies directly from the role's outcomes and must-haves. Each must be observable and job-relevant ("designs schemas that survive scale," not "smart"). Drop anything you cannot tie to a JD requirement. Avoid "culture fit"; if culture matters, decompose it into named, assessable behaviors like "gives direct, kind feedback."

## Weight by Impact on Success
Assign each competency a weight that reflects how much it predicts performance in this specific role, summing to 100%. Core technical/role skills usually outweigh nice-to-haves. Document the rationale so weights are defensible and consistent across the candidate slate. Never weight proxies for protected traits.

## Define an Anchored Scale
Use a 1-4 scale with written behavioral anchors per competency: what a 1 looks like, what a 4 looks like, with concrete evidence examples. Anchors are what turn a vibe into a rating. Require a free-text evidence note for each score so reviewers cite what they observed, not how they felt.

## Map Competencies to Evidence Sources
For each competency, specify where it will be assessed (resume screen, take-home, specific interview) so it is measured once, well, by the right person. Avoid scoring the same trait five times, which over-weights first impressions and amplifies bias.

## Set the Decision Rule Up Front
Define the weighted threshold for advancing before reviewing candidates, and require minimum scores on must-have competencies. Pre-committing the bar prevents post-hoc rationalization. A low overall with one dazzling area should be a conversation, not an automatic pass or fail.

## Fairness and Compliance Bounds
The rubric assists human judgment; it does not auto-advance or auto-reject anyone. Score only job-relevant criteria; never include or infer age, gender, race, disability, or background. Apply the same rubric and anchors to every candidate for the role. Flag any competency that cannot be defended as job-related, and keep scoring records for consistency review.

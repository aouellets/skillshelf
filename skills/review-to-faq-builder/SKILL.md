# Review-to-FAQ Builder

Your customers already wrote your highest-converting copy; it's buried in the reviews and questions. Reviews reveal the exact doubts that stop strangers from buying and the exact words that reassure them. The job is to mine that signal and turn it into an FAQ that answers the hesitation before the shopper even forms it. Ask for the review and Q&A export, plus the return and shipping policy.

## Cluster the Recurring Doubts
Read for patterns, not anecdotes. Group raw reviews into themes: sizing/fit, durability over time, setup difficulty, compatibility, value vs price, smell/taste/material, shipping speed, return ease. Count frequency. The top 5-8 clusters by volume become your FAQ. A doubt that appears once is noise; a doubt that appears twenty times is killing conversions silently.

## Turn Negative Reviews Into Honest Answers
The one- and two-star reviews are gold because they name the deal-breaker objection out loud. Don't hide them; answer them. If reviewers say "runs small," the FAQ says "Does it run small? Yes, many customers size up; see our fit guide." Honest pre-emption converts skeptics and slashes returns. Never write a FAQ answer that contradicts what reviews actually report.

## Write Questions in the Customer's Words
Phrase each question the way a shopper would type it, using the actual vocabulary from reviews ("Will this fit a queen mattress?" not "Dimensional compatibility"). This matches voice search and "People also ask" queries, earning extra search visibility. Keep answers to 2-4 sentences, lead with the direct yes/no/number, then the supporting detail.

## Quote Real Customers as Proof
Where a review answers a question better than you can, lightly quote it (attributed as a verified buyer, lightly edited for clarity, never fabricated). Social proof from a peer outperforms brand assurance. Only use real quotes; never invent a testimonial.

## Order by Conversion Impact
Lead with the objection that most often blocks the sale, not the most common compliment. Put fit, function, and "will it work for me" near the top; logistics (shipping, returns) lower. End with a reassurance line tied to the actual policy.

## When to Push Back
If the review set is tiny or unrepresentative, say so and recommend gathering more before publishing claims. If a review reveals a real product defect, flag it for the product team rather than papering over it with clever copy.

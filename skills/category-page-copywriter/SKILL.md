# Category Page Copywriter

A category page has a harder job than a PDP: it must rank for a broad commercial query ("women's merino base layers") while helping a shopper narrow toward a product fast. The copy can't push products below the fold or read like a keyword salad. Ask for the category, the primary and secondary keywords, the products it contains, and the buyer's main decision criteria.

## Respect the Two-Audience, Above-Fold Rule
Google wants substantive text; shoppers want to see products. Resolve this with a tight intro above the grid (2-3 sentences, ~40-60 words) that states what the collection is and who it's for, then move the longer SEO content to a section below the product grid. Never wall off the products behind a long essay; that tanks engagement and conversions.

## Target Commercial Intent, Not Just Volume
The searcher here is closer to buying than a blog reader. Use the head keyword once naturally in the intro and the H1, then write supporting copy that answers buying questions: how to choose, what differs between options, what fits which use case. This captures "best," "vs," and "for [use case]" long-tail queries that signal intent. Don't repeat the keyword mechanically; semantic coverage beats density.

## Help Shoppers Self-Segment
The most valuable category copy is a buying guide in miniature. Add a short "How to choose" block: 3-4 criteria that route the shopper to the right subset ("Pick the 150 weight for high-output activity, the 250 for cold-weather layering"). This reduces bounce and increases the odds they reach a product confident enough to buy.

## Keep It Scannable and Honest
Use a clear H1, short paragraphs, and subheads for the lower content. Bold the decision criteria. Don't overpromise selection ("hundreds of styles" when there are nine) and don't claim authority or rankings you can't back. Match brand voice exactly.

## Internal Linking and Structure
Link to relevant sibling categories and key PDPs in the lower content using descriptive anchor text, supporting both SEO and navigation. Suggest an FAQ schema block for the lower section to capture "People also ask."

## When to Push Back
If the category is thin (few products) or overlaps another category, flag it: thin category pages rarely rank and can cannibalize. Recommend merging or expanding before investing in copy.

# Abandoned Cart Sequence

Most cart abandonment isn't rejection; it's distraction, comparison shopping, or a small unresolved doubt. A good recovery sequence reminds, reassures, and only then incentivizes, so you don't hand a discount to people who would have bought anyway. The job is a 3-email arc with rising urgency and careful incentive logic. Ask for the brand voice, average order value, margin tolerance for discounting, and whether free shipping is available.

## Email 1: The Helpful Reminder (1-4 hours later)
Send within a few hours while intent is warm. Tone is helpful, not salesy: "You left something behind." Show the cart contents with image and name, restate the core benefit in one line, and give a single clear button back to the cart. No discount here; assume the abandon was accidental or a distraction. A subject line naming the product or a friendly nudge beats a generic "Did you forget something?"

## Email 2: The Objection Handler (~24 hours later)
The shopper hesitated for a reason. Address it: surface trust signals (reviews, ratings, guarantee, easy returns, secure checkout), answer the top objection from your FAQ, and add light social proof ("4.8 stars from 2,000 buyers"). Still no discount, or only a free-shipping nudge if margins allow, since shipping cost is the single most common abandonment cause. Keep urgency honest: "Items in your cart are popular" only if true.

## Email 3: The Incentive + Honest Urgency (~48-72 hours later)
Now, if your margin allows, offer the incentive: a modest percentage or fixed amount, or free shipping, framed as a thank-you not a fire sale. Use real urgency: a genuine expiry on the code ("expires in 48 hours") is fine; a fake countdown is not. State the code clearly and link straight to a pre-filled cart. This is the last touch; don't keep emailing the same incentive.

## Incentive Logic That Protects Margin
Discount last, not first, so you don't train customers to abandon for a coupon. Prefer free shipping or a small gift over deep percentage cuts on low-margin items. For high AOV carts, a fixed-dollar incentive often reads better than a percentage. Never discount below your margin floor.

## Honesty and Compliance
No fake stock counts, no fabricated "price increasing" threats, no countdown timers that reset on reload. Include an unsubscribe and respect that these are triggered transactional-adjacent emails; keep frequency reasonable.

## When to Push Back
If the brand routinely discounts in email 1, flag that it erodes margin and trains abandonment. If AOV is low and margins thin, recommend a free-shipping threshold or no incentive over a percentage off.

# Comparison Page Builder

"X vs Y" and "best alternative to X" are the highest-intent queries in commerce; the searcher has a wallet open and is choosing. A comparison page wins them by being genuinely useful and fair, not by trashing the competitor. Credibility is the conversion lever: a shopper who catches you lying about a rival distrusts everything you claim about yourself. Ask for your product, the named competitor(s), and the real, verifiable differences.

## Win Trust by Conceding Real Points
Name at least one thing the competitor does better. A page that says "we win on everything" reads as marketing and converts worse than one that says "if you need X, they're the better pick; if you need Y, we are." Honest concession makes every other claim believable. Then position your genuine strengths against the buyer segment you actually serve best.

## Build a Scannable, Honest Comparison Table
The table is the page's centerpiece. Choose 6-10 criteria that matter to the buying decision (price, key features, support, warranty, ease of use, fit for use case), not vanity specs. Use clear checkmarks, values, or short phrases per cell. Keep competitor data accurate and dated, since specs and prices change and stale or wrong data is both a trust and legal risk. Never fabricate a competitor's number; if unknown, say "varies" or leave it sourced.

## Frame by Use Case, Not Just Features
Above or below the table, write "Choose us if..." and "Choose them if..." blocks. This routes the right buyer to you and the wrong-fit buyer away (which improves retention and reduces refunds). It also captures long-tail intent like "X vs Y for [use case]."

## Optimize for the Commercial Query
Use the natural "X vs Y" phrasing in the H1, title, and intro. Answer the implicit question fast in the first paragraph so it can earn a featured snippet. Add an FAQ covering "Is X or Y better for..." questions. Link to your relevant PDP with descriptive anchor text.

## Stay Legally and Ethically Clean
Comparative claims must be substantiated and accurate; avoid disparagement, trademark misuse in a misleading way, and outdated pricing. Stick to verifiable, factual differences. When in doubt, attribute the source and date.

## When to Push Back
If the requester wants to misstate a competitor's features or hide your weaknesses, refuse: it backfires on trust and risks legal exposure. If your product genuinely loses on the criteria that matter to most searchers, recommend a different angle (a niche use case you win) rather than a losing head-to-head.

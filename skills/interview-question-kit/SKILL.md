# Interview Question Kit
Structured interviews predict performance roughly twice as well as unstructured chats, mostly because every candidate gets the same job-relevant questions scored against pre-defined answers. Your job is to build a question set where each item maps to a competency and comes with answer guidance, so interviewers assess evidence instead of rapport.

## Map Every Question to a Competency
Start from the role's rubric competencies and write 2-3 questions per competency. Never ask a question that does not assess a defined, job-relevant competency. Distribute competencies across the loop so each is probed deeply by one or two interviewers rather than skimmed by all.

## Write Behavioral Questions in STAR Form
Ask for specific past situations: "Tell me about a time you shipped under a hard deadline with incomplete requirements." Past behavior predicts future behavior better than hypotheticals. Include follow-up probes for Situation, Task, Action, and Result, since candidates often narrate the team's work, not their own.

## Add Role-Specific and Work-Sample Items
Pair behavioral questions with realistic job tasks: a design discussion, a code reading, a mock customer email, a prioritization exercise. Work samples are among the most predictive and most defensible signals. Keep them short, role-true, and identical across candidates.

## Define Good, Mixed, and Weak Answers
For each question, write concrete anchors: what a strong answer demonstrates, what a borderline answer looks like, and red flags. Anchors keep scoring honest and consistent across interviewers and reduce the pull of charisma and similarity bias.

## Standardize Delivery
Give interviewers the same questions, order, and time budget per candidate for the same role. Note which follow-ups are allowed. Consistency is what makes comparisons fair; ad-hoc tangents reintroduce the bias structure removes.

## Fairness and Compliance Bounds
Never generate questions touching age, family or marital status, pregnancy, religion, disability, health, national origin, or salary history where restricted. Assess only job-relevant competencies. The interviewer and panel make the decision; these questions and anchors inform, not replace, human judgment. Flag any question a candidate could reasonably read as probing a protected characteristic.

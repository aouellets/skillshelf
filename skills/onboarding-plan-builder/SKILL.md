# Onboarding Plan Builder
The first 90 days set whether a hire ramps, stalls, or leaves. A good plan replaces vague "get up to speed" with concrete, sequenced milestones, named relationships, and explicit success criteria, so both the new hire and manager know what good looks like at each checkpoint. Build the plan from the role's actual outcomes.

## Phase the 90 Days by Cognitive Load
Structure as Learn (0-30), Contribute (31-60), and Own (61-90). Day 30 should be about understanding systems, people, and context; day 60 about shipping scoped work with support; day 90 about owning an area with autonomy. Tie each phase to 2-4 concrete, verifiable milestones drawn from the role's outcomes, not generic checklists.

## Make the First Week Concrete
Specify access and tools provisioned before day one, a buddy or onboarding partner, the people to meet and why, the first small task that produces a real (even tiny) win, and where to find docs. Ambiguity in week one is the most common cause of early disengagement.

## Define Success Criteria Per Phase
For each milestone, state what "done/on track" looks like in observable terms ("merged first PR," "ran a customer call solo," "presented the area roadmap"). Shared, written criteria let the manager give early, fair feedback instead of a vague gut read at day 90.

## Schedule Real Check-Ins
Put recurring 1:1s on the calendar plus explicit 30/60/90 reviews. Each review covers: progress vs. milestones, blockers, what support is needed, and a two-way fit conversation. Make space for the new hire to give feedback on onboarding itself.

## Build in Relationships and Context
List key cross-functional partners, rituals to join, and the implicit norms a newcomer cannot infer. Ramp is as much social and contextual as technical; isolated new hires ramp slower regardless of skill.

## Fairness and Inclusion Bounds
Apply the same structured plan, support, and success criteria to every hire at a given level; inconsistent onboarding produces unfair early performance signals. Calibrate ramp expectations to role and level, never to assumptions about the person. The plan supports the manager's judgment and any accommodation needs; it does not gate employment. Flag where accommodations, visa, or relocation timing may shift the schedule.
